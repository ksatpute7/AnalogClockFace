Day 1: Spelling Pretest

  • Display these words on the board: amount, prowl, appoint, annoy.

  • Ask students what they noticed about the words.

  • Keep their focus on the patterns and sounds of the words, rather than on the meanings.

  • Guide students to notice the different sounds made by the diphthong ou and ow; and oi and oy.

  • Tell students they will now be given a spelling pretest for the spelling pattern they are focusing on this week. The pretest will show what they already know about this spelling pattern and what they need to learn.

  • Give the pretest.

    • amount: It cost a large amount of money for the trip.
    • annoy: Please don’t annoy the dog.
    • appoint: She will appoint someone to be the leader.
    • avoid: We tried to avoid the construction zone.
    • bounce: The children love to bounce on the trampoline.
    • broil: Mom likes to broil our vegetables.
    • choice: You have a choice between milk and juice.
    • employ: We will employ a helper for the classroom.
    • hour: It took us an hour to hike up the hill.
    • poison: This plant can be poison to some animals.
    • proud: Dad is proud of his children.
    • prowl: The cat likes to prowl at night.
    • shower: The rain shower caught us by surprise.
    • thousand: There were at least a thousand ants on the counter top.
    • voyage: The voyage over the ocean took many days.

  • Note that students may also work with these words in a future phonics lesson.

  • Display the correct spellings of the words for the lesson or hand out printed copies of the word list, available in Teacher Support.

  • Have students work with a partner to check their pretests.

  • Once they have checked their lists, have them write the correct spellings on the right side of the paper for any words they misspelled.

  • Then, have students add their replacement words to any blank lines on the right side of the page. Retain a copy of each student’s spelling list. They can handwrite a second list, or you can use available technology to collect copies.

  • This is the first of five spelling lessons, and it is recommended you teach one each day throughout the week. Let students know that for the final spelling lesson on this pattern, the spelling test will include:

    • The words they misspelled today
    • The words they added today (to replace the ones they spelled correctly)
    • Four new words that fit the spelling pattern

  • Let students know when the test will be given. Remind them to take their list of words home to study.

Emerging PIII.5.1Em

Many English language learners (ELLs) will transfer the sounds and symbols of their home language to English. This linguistic knowledge offers both benefits and drawbacks. In some cases, they may know a cognate that assists them. In other cases, the phonological and orthographic transfer will cause spelling errors.

  • In order to assist ELLs, begin with offering a visual of the spelling words when possible. Then, when they take the pre-test, be certain they rewrite the full word for the ones they missed (rather than just noting specific letter errors).
  • To improve and prepare for the test, offer a cloze procedure for support. Pair the ELL with a strong ELA student who is English proficient and have the ELL write the missed word. Then, below the word, have the non-ELL peer write the word again several times with progressively more letters replaced by a blank space. Have the ELL fill in the blanks to complete the word to gradually reach the correct spelling of each word.

Additional activities may be added to the weekly routines to support language development.

  • When introducing new word patterns, enunciate clearly all sounds in each of the words on the list for the week.
  • Model for students how to pronounce each word correctly and how to use each word in a sentence.
  • Help students practice correct pronunciation.
  • Draw students’ attention to the part(s) of each word that follows the spelling or sound pattern for the week.

Use each student’s Individualized Education Program (IEP) as a guide to determine which of the following strategies will provide maximum support throughout the week. Activities may be in addition to or in place of the Spelling Search or other weekly assignments as per student need.

  • For students who require visual support: Using a text size appropriate for student need, write words on large cards and highlight the pattern in each word. Provide students with these cards to study for the week.
  • For students who require memory support: Reduce the number of words per category.
  • For students who require additional time to process: Allow additional time to complete sorts or reduce the number of items to be sorted.
  • For students who require support connecting symbols to sounds: Provide auditory support (though recordings or partnerships) to provide these students with multiple exposures to hear the correct pronunciation of the words.
  • Check in with students throughout the week to provide differentiated support as needed.
