LESSONS 12–14: Gallery

Gallery Overview

Allow students who have a clear understanding of the content in the unit to work on Gallery problems of their choosing. You can use this time to provide additional help to students who need review of the unit's concepts or to assist students who may have fallen behind on work.

Gallery Descriptions

Multiplication Table

Students will use multiplication knowledge to find missing factors and missing products in a mixed-up multiplication table.

New Product

Students will explain what happens to the product when the number of groups goes up by 1.

Rows of Chairs

Students will look at work done by two third-grade students and explain whether or not their work is correct.

Zoe's Letters

Students will determine how many stamps Zoe used by looking at how many stamps are left and how many stamps she had to start with.

Multiplication Poster

Students will make a poster that uses words, numbers, and math diagrams to explain what makes multiplication special.

Multiplication or Not

In this card sort, students will sort situations into those that can be represented with multiplication and those that cannot. Students will write expressions for the pictures in the “multiplication” group and explain why each situation in the “not multiplication” group cannot be represented with a multiplication expression.

New Product

Answers

Answers will vary. Possible Answers.

  1. The number in the square tells how many groups of 5 there are, and the number in the triangle tells how many in all.


    If there is 1 more group of 5, there will be 5 more in all. Every time the number in the square goes up by 1, the number in the triangle will go up by 5.


    You can see that the number in the triangle goes up by 5 each time. For example, 6 × 5 means 6 groups of 5. When you add 1 more group to the 6 groups, it becomes 7 groups of 5. Anytime you add 1 more group of 5, the answer goes up by 5.

    6 × 5 = 30

    7 × 5 = 35

    8 × 5 = 40


    Students may also make a table or a series of arrays to show that when the multiplicand goes up by 1, the multiplier goes up by 5.

Multiplication Poster

Answers

Posters will vary.

Here are the points a good poster may make (examples will vary):

  1. Multiplication is special because it allows you to represent a situation with several groups of the same size in a simple way. Instead of writing 2 + 2 + 2 + 2 + 2 + 2 + 2 + 2, you can write 8 × 2.


    The meaning of the numbers in a multiplication expression is also special. When you add, all the addends represent the same thing—apples or igloos or pencils. The factors in a multiplication expression do not mean the same thing. The first factor tells you how many of the second factor to count. The second factor tells you the group size that is being counted: 9 × 2 = 18 means 9 twos are 18.

  2. You can multiply when you want to know how many in all and when the groups are all the same size. For example, you can use multiplication to find the total number of feet of ribbon when there are 5 pieces that are each 3 feet long.

  3. You cannot multiply when you want to know how many in all and the groups are not all the same size. For example, you have to add when you want to know how many total feet of ribbon there are when there is one 3-foot piece, one 4-foot piece, and one 5-foot piece; you cannot multiply.

  4. Sometimes you can reorganize the groups to make them all the same size. If you want to know how many fish in all and you have 1 tank with 3 fish, 1 tank with 2 fish, and 1 tank with 3 fish, you can move the fish around so that each tank has 2 fish. Then you can multiply. (You can do this in your head; you don’t actually have to move the fish.)

Three Problems

Answers

Answers will vary. Possible answers.

  1. All three problems have the numbers 5 and 7 in them, but only problems A and C can be solved by multiplying 7 × 5. To solve problem B, you have to add 7 + 5.


    Problems A and C are mathematically alike because each asks how many are in 7 groups of 5. They are multiplication problems. Problem B is different because it asks how many apples Denzel has in all when 7 apples and 5 apples are combined. You cannot use multiplication.


    Problems A and C are alike because "7 rows" is like "7 bags," and "5 chairs in each row" is like "5 apples in each bag." Both are multiplication problems. Problem B is not a multiplication problem because the 7 red apples and 5 green apples are two different-sized groups, not 7 groups of 5. It is an addition problem.
