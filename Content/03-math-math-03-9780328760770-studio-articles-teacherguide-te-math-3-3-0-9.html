LESSON 9: Fours Multiplication Table

Lesson Overview

Students begin the lesson by repeating the threes chant. If at all possible, find time outside of math class to recite the twos and fives chants as well.

Students construct the Fours Multiplication Table by building a group of 4 and replicating iterations of that group of 4. Students represent each of the facts in the Fours Multiplication Table by building a model, writing a multiplication equation, and filling in blanks in a sentence.

Students examine what happens to the product if there are 2 more or 2 fewer groups of 4. They also look at patterns in the multiples of 4.

Content Standards

3. OA—Operations and Algebraic Thinking

Represent and solve problems involving multiplication and division.

1. Interpret products of whole numbers, e.g., interpret 5 × 7 as the total number of objects in 5 groups of 7 objects each. For example, describe a context in which a total number of objects can be expressed as 5 × 7.

3. Use multiplication and division within 100 to solve word problems in situations involving equal groups, arrays, and measurement quantities, e.g., by using drawings and equations with a symbol for the unknown number to represent the problem1.

Understand properties of multiplication and the relationship between multiplication and division.

5. Apply properties of operations as strategies to multiply and divide. Examples: If 6 × 4 = 24 is known, then 4 × 6 = 24 is also known. (Commutative property of multiplication.) 3 × 5 × 2 can be found by 3 × 5 = 15, then 15 × 2 = 30, or by 5 × 2 = 10, then 3 × 10 = 30. (Associative property of multiplication.) Knowing that 8 × 5 = 40 and 8 × 2 = 16, one can find 8 × 7 as 8 × (5 + 2) = (8 × 5) + (8 × 2) = 40 + 16= 56. (Distributive property.)

Solve problems involving the four operations, and identify and explain patterns in arithmetic.

9. Identify arithmetic patterns (including patterns in the addition table or multiplication table), and explain them using properties of operations. For example, observe that 4 times a number is always even, and explain why 4 times a number can be decomposed into two equal addends.

Key Concepts

The commutative property of multiplication, sometimes called the “any order” property in elementary school states that a × b = b × a.

Goals and Learning Objectives

  • Investigate groups of 4

  • Discover more properties of multiplication by building the Fours Multiplication Table

  • Understand “4 apples in each box” as a single group of 4

  • Identify patterns in the multiples of 4

  • Consider how the product changes if there are 2 more or 2 fewer groups of 4
