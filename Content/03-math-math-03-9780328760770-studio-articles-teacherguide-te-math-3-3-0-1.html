LESSON 1: How Many Fingers?

Lesson Overview

Students find different ways to represent the number of fingers in the classroom mathematically. They learn that they can represent the number of objects in several groups of equal size with multiplication expressions.

Content Standards

3.OA—Operations and Algebraic Thinking

Represent and solve problems involving multiplication and division.

3. Use multiplication and division within 100 to solve word problems in situations involving equal groups, arrays, and measurement quantities, e.g., by using drawings and equations with a symbol for the unknown number to represent the problem.

Key Concepts

This lesson introduces a new operation: multiplication. The context produces a long and unwieldy addition expression because students need to immediately see the benefits of representing multiplicative situations with multiplication expressions.

Multiplication expressions represent quantities in a new way. In addition, each number represents individual objects. Each object is treated as a countable unit. In multiplication, one factor tells how many iterations or counts of the quantity represented by the other factor are needed. This requires the construction of a new understanding about numbers: a number can refer not just to the individual items in a group but also to the group itself. Many can be one. This notion, that five individual objects—in this case, fingers—can be regarded both as five individual fingers and as one group of fingers, is one of the first ideas students must learn as they begin to understand multiplication.

The idea that numbers can represent both individual items and groups of items is called “unitizing.” When students have learned to unitize, they can consider not only individual objects but also groups of objects.

In this curriculum, as well as in most U.S. curricula, the convention is that the first factor represents the number of groups and the second factor represents the group size. In many other countries, an alternate convention is to represent the group size first and the number of groups second. Some of your students may come from families who have always used the alternate convention. Be sure to tell your students and their families that in this class, the number of groups will be stated first. This recommendation is important because each factor in a multiplication plays a distinct role, and one way to emphasize this special role is to be consistent in how multiplicative situations are represented.

Goals And Learning Objectives

  • Generate interest in learning to multiply

  • Introduce multiplication expressions

  • Write a multiplication expression for a real-world situation

Materials

  • Manipulatives, such as snap cubes or counters
