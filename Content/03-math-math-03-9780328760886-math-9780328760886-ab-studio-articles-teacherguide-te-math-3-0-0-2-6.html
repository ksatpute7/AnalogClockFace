Task 6: Match Riddles With Solutions

Lesson Guide

After students have completed their independent work, have them work with their partner to match riddles with solutions, following the steps you demonstrated in the Opening.

Tell students to complete all of their work in their Notebook. They should save their work—even if it includes crossed-out mistakes. Explain that mistakes represent their thinking. Thinking cannot be erased—it can only be revised and improved upon.

Tell students that when they prepare their presentation they can use their Notebook to clearly present their corrected work next to examples of their mistakes.

SWD:

Be sure that students with disabilities are engaging in the activity successfully. If you find that some students need support, consider grouping those that need extra help and work with them as a way of supporting them.

ELL:

Consider showing these questions in writing so that students can refer back to them should they need to. Make sure to give sentence frames to ELLs to support them in answering the questions.

Mathematical Practices

Mathematical Practice 2: Reason abstractly and quantitatively.

  • Answer questions about numbers using abstract place-value concepts

Mathematical Practice 3: Construct viable arguments and critique the reasoning of others.

  • Students engage in productive partner dialog by presenting their explanations and critiquing each other’s reasoning.

Mathematical Practice 7: Look for and make use of structure.

  • Students identify equivalent groupings of hundreds, tens, and ones.

Interventions

Student is stuck.

  • Try using drawings or a manipulative to help you figure out the riddles.

Student can’t find answers to riddles that only describe the number of tens and ones because student expects that the answer will have only two digits.

  • How many digits are in the number that has 10 tens?

  • What about 20 tens or 38 tens?

Student is confused by riddles that give the number and ask how many hundreds or tens are in the number.

  • Write an expanded form equation to help you understand the riddle.

  • Talk with your partner about your equation.

Answers

  • Card sort solutions.

    I have 23 tens and 6 ones. Who am I? 236
    I have 50 tens and 13 ones. Who am I? 513
    I have 40 tens and 7 ones. Who am I? 407
    I have 10 tens and 500 ones. Who am I? 600
    I am 183. I have 23 ones and 1 hundred. How many tens do I have? 6
    I am 500. I have 9 tens and 10 ones. How many hundreds do I have? 4
    I am 490. I have 18 tens and 10 ones. How many hundreds do I have? 3

  • Circulate the classroom as students work with their partners. Check to see that they are comparing matches and challenging one another to explain differences.
