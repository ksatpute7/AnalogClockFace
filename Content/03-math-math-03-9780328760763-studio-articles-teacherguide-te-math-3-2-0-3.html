Key Concepts

Rounding a number gives a result close to the original number, but with fewer non-zero digits. Rounded numbers are easier to compute with and are useful for estimating the results of calculations.

To round a whole number to the hundreds place, look at the tens place.

  • If the digit in the tens place is 5 or greater, round the digit in the hundreds place up to the next digit and write zeros in the tens and ones places.

  • If the digit in the tens place is less than 5, leave the digit in the hundreds place as it is and write zeros in the tens and ones places.

To round a whole number to the tens place, look at the ones place.

  • If the digit in the ones place is 5 or greater, round the number in the tens place up to the next digit and write a zero in the ones place.

  • If the digit in the ones place is less than 5, leave the digit in the tens place as is and write a zero in the ones place.

Many people think the rule of rounding up when the digit to the right is 5 is an arbitrary convention, but it is actually a logical rule. For example, consider all the numbers between 300 and 400 with 5 in the tens place.

350 351 352 353 354 355 356 357 358 359

Of these ten numbers, nine are closer to 400 than to 300, so rounding up to 400 does make sense.

Goals and Learning Objectives

  • Develop and apply rules for rounding numbers to the nearest hundred

  • Develop and apply rules for rounding numbers to the nearest ten

SWD:

Using approximation and estimation can be challenging for some students with disabilities. Consider reviewing and reinforcing this skill with students prior to having them start the tasks in which they will be expected to approximate/estimate.
