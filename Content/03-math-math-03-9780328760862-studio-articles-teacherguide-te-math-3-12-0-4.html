LESSON 4: How Much Change?

Lesson Overview

Students will use the context of shopping situations to write one equation that can be used to solve a two-step word problem involving calculating change. Students will explore the use of parentheses to combine equations.

Content Standards

3.OA—Operations and Algebraic Thinking

Solve problems involving the four operations, and identify and explain patterns in arithmetic.

8. Solve two-step word problems using the four operations. Represent these problems using equations with a letter standing for the unknown quantity. Assess the reasonableness of answers using mental I computation and estimation strategies including rounding.

Key Concepts

In this lesson, students explore the use of parentheses in writing one equation to represent a two-step problem situation. Specifically, they explore this problem:

Chan has $50. She is buying some presents for her cousins. The teddy bear costs $14, and the basketball costs $28. How much change will Chan get after she pays for the presents?

Solving this problem requires two steps. First, add 14 and 28 to find the total cost of the gifts. Then, subtract this sum from 50 to find how much change Chan gets.

Some students may solve this problem by drawing two separate diagrams and writing two separate expressions or equations:

Step 1 Calculate the total cost of the gifts.

14+28=? 14+28=42

Step 2 Calculate the amount of change.

50−42=? 50−42=8

  • In this lesson, students will learn that they can represent both steps with a single diagram and equation. They will also see that a letter, or variable, can be used to stand for the unknown quantity

50−(14+28)=n

The variable n is used to represent the amount of change Chan will get. The tape diagram shows that the total of $50 is made up of the $14 cost for the teddy bear, the $28 for the basketball, and the change (n). The parentheses are needed in the equation to indicate that the prices are added first and then that sum is subtracted from the total (50).

Goals and Learning Objectives

  • Write equations to find unknown values in word problems

  • Write expressions or equations with parentheses to represent two-step problem situations

  • Model problem situations

  • Estimate solutions

  • Evaluate the accuracy of calculations using the relationship between operations
