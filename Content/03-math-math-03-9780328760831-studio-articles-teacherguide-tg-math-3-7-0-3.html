LESSON 3: How Long Will Each Piece Be?

Lesson Overview

Students chant along with the animation to attain mastery of the fours. They solve a division problem that involves equal shares of a measured length. They prepare a presentation that includes a model of the problem, an equation, and an answer to the problem.

Key Concepts

In equal sharing, or partitive division situations, the number of groups is known, and you divide to find the number in each group. In this lesson, students grapple with quantities that must be measured rather than counted.

This is a typical equal-shares math story involving measured lengths:

  • Ms. Perkins has 12 feet of ribbon.

  • She wants to use an equal amount of ribbon to wrap 4 packages.

  • How much ribbon can she use for each package?

Acting out this problem might involve an action such as folding the ribbon in half, and then folding it in half again to get four equal lengths. To calculate the length of one piece, divide the total by 4. The quotient, 3, tells how much ribbon can be used for each package.

As with sharing countable objects, the total and the number of groups are known. The problem asks how much is in each group. That question can be answered by asking:

  • 4 times what number makes 12?

Or:

4×☐=12
SWD:

Many students with disabilities have kinesthetic learning styles. Inviting them to act out the situations will help them conceptualize the context of division. Have twelve students arrange themselves in four equal groups.

Partitive division can be difficult for struggling mathematicians. They tend to begin with a trial and error method for solving so starting with concrete cubes and attaching a visual representation to the problem will help guide them toward greater efficiency. Students can use the visual model to put the 12 cubes into the four groups, counting the cubes out much like you would hand out a deck of cards. The goal, over time, is for students to internalize the visual representation and move to the abstract with an equation 12÷4=☐ .

ELL:

When introducing new words, remember to allow ELLs to use a dictionary. Also, repeat the new words at a slower pace, write them down, and ask some of the students to repeat after you. Be sure ELLs feel comfortable with the pronunciation.


Goals and Learning Objectives

  • Model equal shares of measured lengths

  • Understand the meaning of the dividend, divisor, and quotient in sharing measurement situations

  • Understand sharing division as an unknown multiplicand problem

  • Write division and multiplication equations for a situation

Materials

  • Optional: Pieces of string 32 inches long, for students to act out sharing the string
