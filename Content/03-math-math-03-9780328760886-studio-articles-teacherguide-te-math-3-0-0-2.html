LESSON 2: Matching Amounts

Lesson Overview

Students begin by reviewing the routines in a lesson: Opening, Work Time, Ways of Thinking, Summary of the Math, Reflection, and Exercises. They create a list of classroom expectations that describe guidelines for making each math class run smoothly. Students discuss what makes a good presentation, and how to effectively work with a partner. They use the ideas from the discussion to work with a partner to match number riddles with solutions.

Content Standards

This unit focuses on prerequisites of the following standards.

3. NBT—Number and Operations in Base Ten

Use place value understanding and properties of operations to perform multi-digit arithmetic.

2. Fluently add and subtract within 1,000 using strategies and algorithms based on place value, properties of operations, and/or the relationship between addition and subtraction.

3. Multiply one-digit whole numbers by multiples of 10 in the range 10–90 (e.g., 9×80, 5 ×60) using strategies based on place value and properties of operations.

Key Concepts

Students solve riddles that break down numbers in different ways.

Goals and Learning Objectives

  • Learn about the routines in a lesson: Opening, Work Time, Ways of Thinking, Summary of the Math, Reflection, and Exercises

  • Generate a list of classroom expectations

  • Discuss what makes a good presentation

  • Collaborate with a partner

  • Critique a partner’s reasoning

  • Understand that a grouping of hundreds, tens, and ones can be decomposed and recomposed to make different, equivalent groupings

  • Think flexibly about three-digit numbers and understand a number can be represented in different ways

Materials

  • Chart paper and markers to make Classroom Routines, Expectations, and Good Presentations, and Partner Work posters

Preparation

  • Create a Math Class Routines chart and post in the classroom, such as the one below. The chart helps students learn the classroom routines. Consider adding visuals or color-coding each section to help all students become familiar with the class routines.

Math Class Routines

  • Opening

    • Spark your thinking about the math in the lesson

    • Listen

    • Turn and talk

    • Whole-class discussion

  • Work Time

    • Solve one or more problems on your own and with your partner

    • Work quietly

    • Talk with a partner

    • Talk in small groups

    • Prepare a presentation

  • Ways of Thinking

    • Present and discuss your mathematical thinking from Work Time.

    • Explain your thinking

    • Listen to your classmates

    • Offer suggestions

    • Ask questions

    • Make comments about your classmates’ solutions

  • Summary of the Math

    • Read and discuss a summary

    • Write a summary

  • Reflection

    • Think back on the lesson and write questions or ideas about the lesson

    • Write quietly

  • Exercises

    • Work through additional problems that build on the mathematics in the lesson

    • Work quietly

    • Continue working when struggling

    • Check your own work

  • Decide on your classroom expectations for the year related to gathering materials as start math class, putting away materials at end of the lesson, when and how to submit exercises, and any other rituals to help the class run smoothly. Choose classroom expectations that work for your classroom and teaching style. The goal is for students to move smoothly into each part of the lesson in a consistent way. Consider making a poster to give students a visual reminder of the expectations.

  • Make a Partner Work poster to post in the classroom. An example is shown below, but adjust wording to reflect the classroom norms agreed on during Lesson 1.

Partner Work

  • Say what you think you need to do

  • Show what you wrote or what you think you should write

  • Listen to each other carefully

  • Compare your work

  • Ask questions so you understand each other’s reasoning

  • Explain your reasons for disagreeing with their partner’s work

  • Finish the problem together

  • Check to see if their answers are clear and complete

  • Take a photo of the posters to share with the students.
