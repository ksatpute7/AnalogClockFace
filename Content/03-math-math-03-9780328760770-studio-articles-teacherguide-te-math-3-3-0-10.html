LESSON 10: Groups of 4

Lesson Overview

Students repeat the fours chant during the Opening. Find a few minutes outside of math class to recite the threes, twos, and fives chants as well.

Students consider a simple case of the commutative property in a concrete context of two arrangements of plants. They also practice representing multiplication in different ways (in equal groups, with a tape diagram, and in an array) and justify how each representation shows 6 × 4.

Key Concepts

Arrays are first introduced in Lesson 8 as an arrangement of objects in rows and columns. To maintain a parallel structure to that of equal-sized groups, the convention of representing the number of rows with the first factor and the number of objects in each row (or the number of columns) with the second factor is continued.

This is the first lesson to give students an opportunity to investigate the commutative property. Do not expect that this one lesson will cement students’ understanding of this property. There will be plenty of other opportunities to revisit this property in the Multiplication II and Mastering Multiplication units. For now, students should recognize that the number of tomato plants in 3 rows of 4 is the same as the number of tomato plants in 4 rows of 3. Visualizing the arrangements of plants can help.

If the model of 3 rows of 4 is turned, the array becomes 4 rows of 3 without changing the total number of plants.

Goals and Learning Objectives

  • Learn the fours chant to help memorize the fours facts

  • Solve a problem involving groups of “4 plants”

  • Understand that when a 4 × 3 array is rotated, it becomes a 3 × 4 array and that the number of objects in the array does not change

  • SWD:

    Circle groups of objects in two different arrays in different-sized groups to visually show the commutative properties of multiplication. Then have students help you write the equations that go with the arrays.
