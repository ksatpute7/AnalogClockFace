Type of Unit: Concept

Length of Unit: 15 days

Prior Knowledge

Students should be able to:

  • Interpret products of whole numbers as the total in some number of equal groups.

  • Apply properties of operations as strategies for multiplication and division.

  • Find the perimeter of polygons.

  • Measure length by selecting and using an appropriate tool such as a ruler, yardstick, meter stick, or measuring tape.

  • Partition a rectangle into rows and columns of same-size squares and count to find the total number of them.

Lesson Flow

The unit begins with an exploratory lesson in which students explore ways to compare the areas of two rectangles directly by placing one on top of the other.

Over the course of the next two lessons, students move from directly comparing two areas to indirectly comparing them by first finding the area of each rectangle. In order to indirectly compare areas, students investigate different ways to find area. Students learn that area can be measured by finding the number of unit squares that exactly cover a figure with no gaps and no overlaps.

In Lesson 3, students are introduced to the square centimeter. They learn that a square centimeter is a square that is 1 cm long and 1 cm wide. They learn that this square is 1 square centimeter (1 cm2). Students begin to use this standard unit to measure the area of rectangles.

In Lessons 4 and 5, students are asked to devise methods for finding the areas of rectangles. In Lesson 5, students learn that they can find area by multiplying the length and the width. Lesson 6 connects finding area to the multiplication work students have done and gives them an opportunity to think explicitly about how they can use the properties of multiplication to find the area of rectangles.

Next, students are tasked with finding the area of rectilinear figures. Since the formulas that they have developed so far can be used only for rectangles, students will need to devise strategies for finding the area of a variety of rectilinear figures.

Finally, students investigate figures that have the same area and different perimeters. They also investigate figures that have the same perimeter and different areas. Students find all possible perimeters for rectangles with equal area and then they find possible areas for figures with the same perimeters.

The lessons are followed by a two-day Gallery in which students can choose from among a menu of area tasks. The unit ends with a final assessment.

Contents

  1. Lesson 1: Which Covers More Area? 8

    Students begin to explore the concept of area as a measurement by comparing the area of two figures.

  2. Lesson 2: Which Has the Larger Area? 14

    Students compare the area of rectangles using squares. They discuss the advantage of using squares as a unit versus other arbitrary units.

  3. Lesson 3: Square Centimeters 21

    Students are introduced to square centimeters, a unit of measurement for area. They find the area of figures and draw figures with a given area.

  4. Lesson 4: Area of Rectangles 29

    Students explore developing different strategies to find the area of rectangles.

  5. Lesson 5: More Area of Rectangles 36

    Students find the area of rectangles that have length and width labeled.

  6. Lesson 6: Multiplication and Area 43

    Students further explore finding the area of rectangles to see examples of the properties of multiplication.

  7. Lesson 7: Area of Rectilinear Figures

    Students explore strategies for finding the area of rectilinear figures.

  8. Lesson 8: The Area of Large Figures 60

    Students find the areas of large figures using larger units.

  9. Lesson 9: Eight Square Units 66

    Students draw figures with an area of eight square units. They also find the area of different figures.

  10. Lesson 10: Perimeters of Figures 74

    Students explore the perimeters of rectangles with the same area.

  11. Lesson 11: Equal Perimeters 82

    Students explore the area of rectangles with the same perimeter. They will complete the Self Check task.

  12. Lesson 12: Putting It Together 93

    Students review and revise their work on the Self Check task. They critique samples of student work.

  13. Lessons 13–14: Gallery 100

    Students who have a clear understanding of the content thus far in the unit will work on Gallery problems of their choosing.

  14. Lesson 15: Unit Assessment 105

    The final assessment tests students on expressions, equations, and inequalities.

Common Core State Standards for Mathematics

This unit focuses on the following Common Core State Standards for Mathematics:

3.MD—Measurement and Data

Geometric measurement: understand concepts of area and relate area to multiplication and addition.

5. Recognize area as an attribute of plane figures and understand concepts of area measurement.

a. A square with side length 1 unit, called “a unit square,” is said to have “one square unit” of area, and can be used to measure area.

b. A plane figure that can be covered without gaps or overlaps by n unit squares is said to have an area of n square units.

6. Measure areas by counting unit squares (square cm, square m, square in, square ft, and improvised units).

7. Relate area to the operations of multiplication and addition.

a. Find the area of a rectangle with whole-number side lengths by tiling it, and show that the area is the same as would be found by multiplying the side lengths.

b. Multiply side lengths to find areas of rectangles with whole number side lengths in the context of solving real world and mathematical problems, and represent whole-number products as rectangular areas in mathematical reasoning.

c. Use tiling to show in a concrete case that the area of a rectangle with whole-number side lengths a and b + c is the sum of a × b and a × c. Use area models to represent the distributive property in mathematical reasoning.

d. Recognize area as additive. Find areas of rectilinear figures by decomposing them into non-overlapping rectangles and adding the areas of the non-overlapping parts, applying this technique to solve real world problems.

Geometric measurement: recognize perimeter as an attribute of plane figures and distinguish between linear and area measures.

  • 8. Solve real world and mathematical problems involving perimeters of polygons, including finding the perimeter given the side lengths, finding an unknown side length, and exhibiting rectangles with the same perimeter and different areas or with the same area and different perimeters.

Mathematical Practices

  1. Make sense of problems and persevere in solving them.

  2. Reason abstractly and quantitatively.

  3. Construct viable arguments and critique the reasoning of others.

  4. Model with mathematics.

  5. Use appropriate tools strategically.

  6. Attend to precision.

  7. Look for and make use of structure.

  8. Look for and express regularity in repeated reasoning.
