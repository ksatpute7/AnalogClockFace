Task 2: Reread Boxes for Katje

    • Tell students that strong readers reread texts as if they were investigators. This means that they look very closely at the text so that they are able to answer questions about the story and characters.

      Emerging P1.3.6

      Have Emerging ELLs reread the text with a partner as you support them. Students can preview the questions they must answer before rereading closely. Have students describe the communities using sentence frames:

      • Rosie’s letters are _____.
      • Katje’s letters are _____.
      • Rosie’s community worked together by _____.

      Students can look back at the images to help them derive the language.

      Expanding P1.3.6

      Have Expanding ELLs reread the text with a partner as you provide moderate support. Students can read closely, and each partner can be in charge of describing Rosie and Katje’s letters. Have each partner look closely at one letter and then describe it using details from the text. Have students describe the communities using sentence frames:

      • Rosie’s letters are _____.
      • Katje’s letters are _____.
      • Rosie’s community achieved more by working together because _____.

      Bridging P1.3.6

      Have Bridging ELLs reread the text with a partner as you provide light support. Students can read closely and share evidence from the text to answer the questions.

    • Set up partners for this task. Ask students to reread the letters from Rosie and Katje in Boxes for Katje. These are spread across the text. One student will read Rosie’s letters and the other will read Katje’s letters.

    • Use the annotation to help students understand the text.

    • The objective of this reread is to help students get a deeper sense of the concept of community. Guide them to see how Rosie’s letters got longer as the transatlantic friendship grew and as Rosie became more familiar with the suffering of the Dutch community.

      Check to ensure that students have access to the big ideas from this activity. It may be necessary for these ideas to be presented in multiple ways (auditory and visual) and for them to be repeated and reinforced.

    • Make sure students understand what is being asked by the question “How did Rosie’s community achieve more by working together?”

    • When students are done reading, have them share the evidence they found to answer the question.

    • Ask how Rosie’s letters changed over time. (They got longer and more descriptive.)

    • Reread Rosie’s first and then final letter with students.

    • Ask students:

      • How is her last letter different from her first letter? (In the first letter, it was only Rosie and her mother sending items to Katje. In the last letter, it was the whole community!)
