LESSON 3: Beyond the Hundred Chart

Lesson Overview

Students look for patterns in the hundred chart and fill in missing numbers in similar number charts. They learn to use “ask myself” questions to understand a problem before solving it, and discuss some of the things they can do if they get stuck on a problem.

Content Standards

This unit focuses on prerequisites of the following standards:

3. OA—Operations and Algebraic Thinking

Solve problems involving the four operations, and identify and explain patterns in arithmetic.

9. Identify arithmetic patterns (including patterns in the addition table or multiplication table), and explain them using properties of operations. For example, observe that 4 times a number is always even, and explain why 4 times a number can be decomposed into two equal addends.

Key Concepts

In this lesson students count within 1,000 and read and write numbers to 1,000 using base-ten numerals, number names, and expanded form. Students use patterns in the hundred chart to make sense of place value.

Goals and Learning Objectives

  • Make sense of a problem using “ask myself” questions

  • Identify things to do if they get stuck so they can persevere in solving a problem

  • Understand the structure of written numbers in our place value system

  • Read, write, and say numbers to 1,000

  • Understand the role of 0 as a placeholder

  • Recognize number patterns

Materials

  • Chart paper and markers to make Working Alone, Asking Myself Questions, and What To Do If You Get Stuck posters

Preparation

  • Make a Work Alone poster to post in the classroom. An example is show below, but adjust wording to reflect the classroom norms agreed on during Lesson 1.

    Work Alone:
    What I See— What I Hear—
    ∙ Students working by themselves ∙ Very quiet
    ∙ Students stay in their seats ∙ You might hear soft music
    What I Do—
    ∙ Read the problem
    ∙ Think about the math
    ∙ Start solving the problem
    ∙ Use manipulatives to model the problem
    ∙ Sketch diagrams
    ∙ Look at past lessons for ideas
    ∙ Look at charts around the room
    ∙ Write what you know
    ∙ Write questions to ask later

  • Take a photo of the Work Alone poster to share with the students.
