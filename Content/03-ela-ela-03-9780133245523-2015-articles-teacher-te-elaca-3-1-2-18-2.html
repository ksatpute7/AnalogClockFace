Task 2: Editing Strategies

    • Be sure students are clear in their definition of editing. Provide more information about this important part of writing, if necessary. Be sure that students understand that editing is vital in making sure our written messages come across to the reader as intended!

    • Ask students to think about their experiences with editing and have them share what they did to edit their work.

    • Begin a chart titled “Editing Strategies Checklist.” Ask students to contribute their thoughts and chart their responses. Your chart might look similar to this one below. It is also available as a printable in Teacher Support.

      Editing Strategies Checklist

      Read your piece again.

      • Does your piece make sense?
      • Is each sentence a complete sentence?

      Check punctuation.

      • Does each sentence have an end mark?
      • Have you used commas in the right places?
      • Did you add quotation marks to any dialogue?

      Check capital letters.

      • Do you have a capital letter at the beginning of each sentence?
      • Is the word “I” capitalized?
      • Did you capitalize proper nouns (the names of people and places)?
      • Did you capitalize the months of the year and days of the week?

      Check spelling.

      • Did you spell the Word Wall words correctly?
      • Did you use technology wisely to correct your spelling?
      • Did you use resources to check words you thought were incorrect?

    • When the chart is complete, display it in the classroom so that it is available for students to see. Students may want to take a picture of the chart for future reference.

      Emerging P1.3.5

      Have Emerging ELLs demonstrate active listening skills during the class discussion by prompting students with yes-no and wh- questions to elicit the various editing strategies. Speak at a slower pace and allow sufficient wait-time for an ELL to respond. As each category is written on the checklist, create a visual representation that will help students make connections between the words and the concept. For example, next to the “Read your piece again,” insert a picture of a student reading the piece.

      Expanding P1.3.5

      Have Expanding ELLs demonstrate active listening skills during the class discussion by answering detailed questions about the editing strategies with occasional prompting and moderate support. Speak at a natural pace and allow some wait-time while the student gathers his or her thoughts.

      Bridging P1.3.5

      Have Bridging ELLs demonstrate active listening skills during the class discussion by answering detailed questions about the editing strategies with minimal prompting and light support. Allow some wait-time while the student gathers his or her thoughts.
