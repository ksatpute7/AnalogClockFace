LESSON 4: The Number of Equal Groups

Lesson Overview

Students chant along with the multiplication animations. They solve division problems that involve finding the number of equal groups. They prepare presentations that include a model of the problem, a division equation, a multiplication equation, and a complete answer to the problem.

Key Concepts

In measurement or equal-groups situations, the size of the portion that is distributed to each group is known, and we calculate to find the number of groups. In this lesson, students divide countable objects to find the number of equal groups.

This is a typical equal-groups math story:

There are 12 plums. Mrs. Anderson wants to put 4 plums in each lunch bag. How many lunch bags can get plums?

Acting out this problem might involve an action such as scooping up an equal number of items (in this case, 4 plums) until there are not enough items left to make another scoop. The diagram below shows how you might make groups of 4 plums. The numbers on the plums indicate the order in which they were grouped.

The total and the number in each group are known. The problem asks how many groups there will be. That question can be answered by asking, “What number times 4 makes 12?” Or ☐×4=12 ?

  • ELL:

    When introducing new words or using words that students recently learned, whose meaning they may have forgotten, repeat the new words at a slower pace and write them down if possible. Ask some of the students to repeat after you. Be sure ELLs feel comfortable with the pronunciation. Additionally, allow ELLs to use a dictionary.

  • SWD:

    Kinesthetic learners benefit greatly from acting out problems using concrete manipulatives. If you think some of your mathematicians will struggle with this concept, pulling them to front load this learning will be advantageous. Encourage students to record a visual representation of the situation after they've worked with the concrete model. Then scaffold their learning further and taking them to the abstract by having them record the equation that best represents the context of the problem.
