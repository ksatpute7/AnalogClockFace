LESSON 2: What Is Multiplication?

Lesson Overview

Students are introduced to the meaning of each factor in a multiplication expression, and they learn to differentiate between situations that are multiplicative and situations that are not multiplicative.

Content Standards

3.OA—Operations and Algebraic Thinking

Represent and solve problems involving multiplication and division.

1. Interpret products of whole numbers, e.g., interpret 5×7 as the total number of objects in 5 groups of 7 objects each. For example, describe a context in which a total number of objects can be expressed as 5×7 .

3. Use multiplication and division within 100 to solve word problems in situations involving equal groups, arrays, and measurement quantities, e.g., by using drawings and equations with a symbol for the unknown number to represent the problem.

Key Concepts

Up to this point, students have been thinking about numbers as a way of counting individual items. They are comfortable with counting 5 fingers or 3 kittens. When students use addition to represent, for example, the total number of kittens in 4 cages with 3 kittens in each cage ( 3+3+3+3 ), each 3 is a count of kittens; the kittens are treated as countable units. When using multiplication to represent the same situation ( 4×3 ), the factor 4 takes on an entirely different meaning. Rather than counting individual kittens, it counts groups of kittens.

The order of factors is a matter of convention. In this curriculum, the convention is to put the number of groups first and the number in each group second.

The meaning of the factors is not just a matter of convention. In multiplication, one factor tells the number of iterations, or counts, of the quantity represented by the other factor. Focus on the units! The first factor represents the number of groups. The second factor represents the number in each group.

  • SWD:

    Encourage students to say, “groups of” when they are reading a multiplication expression. For example 6 x 4 is read, “Six groups of four.” This vocabulary helps clarify the mental image.

Goals and Learning Objectives

  • Practice writing multiplication expressions

  • Focus on the special meaning of each factor (the first factor represents the number of groups, and the second factor represents the number in each group)

  • Understand when multiplication is appropriate and when it is not
