Work Time

Task 4: Independent Reading

    • Give students time to select one or two books for Independent Reading. Tell them that once they choose their books, they should begin reading.

      Ask students to show you their use of the Five-Finger Rule for the book they selected. If they have picked a book too far above or below their reading level or seem confused about the rule, guide them toward a “just-right” book.

      Emerging P1.3.3

      Have Emerging ELLs orally restate the Five-Finger Rule. Provide students with a handout of the Five-Finger Rule to serve as a personal checklist.

      Have students state their opinion on why they chose the book. If you notice they have picked a book too far above or too far below their reading level or if they seem confused about the rule, guide them toward a “just-right” book.

      Expanding P1.3.3

      Have Expanding ELLs restate the Five-Finger Rule. Provide students with a handout of the Five-Finger Rule to serve as a personal checklist.

      Ask them to explain how they applied the Five-Finger Rule to their book and to give their opinion as to why the book is appropriate. They can use the vocabulary, phrases, sentences, and visuals to tell you what they did. Then, they should focus on reading independently.

      Bridging P1.3.3

      Provide Bridging ELLs with a handout of the Five-Finger Rule to serve as a personal checklist. Ask them to give their opinion on why the book is appropriate for them. Ensure ELLs at this level have chosen a sufficiently challenging book. Encourage rigor.

      Additional Materials

      Five-Finger Rule handout

      ELL Task Preparation

      Create a handout of the Five-Finger Rule.

    • Although your goal is for students to read for 30 minutes each day, begin with 15–20 minutes and monitor to identify any students who may have difficulty maintaining a focus on their reading.

    • While students read, look over the shoulder of each student and make note of the title and level of the text he/she is reading. This quick check will alert you to students who have chosen books that are either too hard or too easy. You may also spot students who have chosen a book that will be difficult for them due to the lack of prior knowledge of the subject.

    • Ask two or three students to read a paragraph or two to you; note if they chose a book with the appropriate level of challenge. You may need to bring students who are choosing books that are too easy or too hard together for small-group instruction on strategies for choosing a book that is just right.
