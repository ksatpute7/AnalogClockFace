Type of Unit: Introduction

Length of Unit: 5 days

Prior Knowledge

Students should be able to:

  • Understand that the three digits of a three-digit number represent amounts of hundreds, tens, and ones

  • Read and write numbers to 1,000 using base-10 numerals, number names, and expanded form

  • Describe patterns in the hundred chart and other number charts

  • Mentally add 1 or 10 to a given number 100–900, and mentally subtract 1 or 10 from a given number 100–900 

  • Count by 2s, 5s, and 10s

Lesson Flow

This unit introduces students to the rituals and routines that build a successful classroom math community. It also introduces the basic features of the application that students will use throughout the year.

Lesson 1 begins with an Icebreaker activity in which students match number cards with expanded form cards; they use their results to determine their partner for the week. Over the course of the week, students focus on the rituals and routines of the program. They learn about the routines in each lesson: Opening, Work Time, Ways of Thinking, Apply the Learning (some lessons), Summary of the Math, Reflection, and Exercises. Students discuss classroom norms and expectations and how to take responsibility for their own learning. They learn how to present their work to the class, how to work with a partner, how to be a productive listener during presentations, the types of questions to ask presenters, and how to work on their own by using “ask myself” questions and resources if they get stuck.

In the last lesson, students talk about the importance of taking responsibility for their own learning. Students learn about the Gallery and how to choose a Gallery problem. They will choose one of three Gallery problems that introduce the unit’s technology resources and deal with the place-value concepts students learned in the unit.

The mathematical work of the unit focuses on place value. Students work with the expanded form of three-digit numbers and solve place-value riddles. They explore patterns in the hundred chart and use the patterns to find missing numbers and skip-count by 5s or 10s.

Contents

  • Lesson 1: Numbers in Expanded Form 7

    Students will determine the partner they will work with for the week by matching cards that show either a numeral or expanded form. The class will make a list of classroom norms. Students will learn the basic features of the tablet as they work on a problem about expanded form.

  • Lesson 2: What Number Am I? 12

    Students will learn about the routines in a lesson: Opening, Work Time, Ways of Thinking, Apply the Learning (some lessons), Summary of the Math, Reflection, and Exercises. They will create a list of classroom expectations that describe guidelines for making each math class run smoothly. Students will discuss what makes a good presentation, and how to effectively work with a partner. They will use the ideas from the discussion to work with a partner to match number riddles with solutions.

  • Lesson 3: Beyond the Hundred Chart 19

    Students will look for patterns in the hundred chart and fill in missing numbers in similar number charts. They will learn to use “ask myself” questions to understand a problem before solving it, and discuss some of the things they can do if they get stuck on a problem.

  • Lesson 4: Skip-Counting to 1,000 27

    Students will discuss the important ways that listeners contribute to the mathematical discussions that occur during Ways of Thinking presentations. They will make a list of the types of questions they can ask presenters. Students will look for and use number patterns to skip-count by 5s and 10s.

  • Lesson 5: Participating in a Gallery 36

    Students will discuss the ways that classroom habits and routines can help them meet their responsibility as a math student. They will learn what a Gallery is and how to choose a Gallery problem to work on. Then they will choose one of three Gallery problems that introduce the unit’s technology resources and deal with the place-value concepts students learned in the unit.

  • Lesson 6: Gallery 53

    This is really just a part of Lesson 5 in this unit. In future units, Galleries will be separate, multi-day enrichment lessons. Students who are caught up will work on these to give you additional time to review with other students.

  • Additional Materials 56

    Additional materials (e.g., exercises, printables like the Icebreaker Activity, guidelines) are available in the Prepare Your Lesson section of Teacher Support. Some of these resources may be required for this unit. Teacher Support is available via the System Tray or by logging in to Teacher Support at teachersupport.pearsonsystemofcourses.com.

Common Core State Standards for Mathematics

This unit focuses on prerequisites of the following Common Core State Standards:

  • 3.OA—Operations and Algebraic Thinking

    Represent and solve problems involving multiplication and division.

    3. Use multiplication and division within 100 to solve word problems in situations involving equal groups, arrays, and measurement quantities, e.g., by using drawings and equations with a symbol for the unknown number to represent the problem.

    Solve problems involving the four operations, and identify and explain patterns in arithmetic.

    9. Identify arithmetic patterns (including patterns in the addition table or multiplication table), and explain them using properties of operations. For example, observe that 4 times a number is always even, and explain why 4 times a number can be decomposed into two equal addends.

  • 3.NBT—Number and Operations in Base Ten

    • Use place value understanding and properties of operations to perform multi-digit arithmetic.

    • 1. Use place value understanding to round whole numbers to the nearest 10 or 100. 

    • 2. Fluently add and subtract within 1000 using strategies and algorithms based on place value, properties of operations, and/or the relationship between addition and subtraction. 

    • 3. Multiply one-digit whole numbers by multiples of 10 in the range 10–90 (e.g., 9 × 80, 5 × 60) using strategies based on place value and properties of operations.

Mathematical Practices

  1. Make sense of problems and persevere in solving them.

  2. Reason abstractly and quantitatively.

  3. Construct viable arguments and critique the reasoning of others.

  4. Model with mathematics.

  5. Use appropriate tools strategically.

  6. Attend to precision.

  7. Look for and make use of structure.

  8. Look for and express regularity in repeated reasoning.
