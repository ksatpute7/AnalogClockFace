LESSON 9: How Much Money?

Lesson Overview

Students will write an equation to represent a two-step problem. In the equation, the unknown may not be isolated on one side of the equation.

Content Standards

3.OA—Operations and Algebraic Thinking

Solve problems involving the four operations, and identify and explain patterns in arithmetic.

8. Solve two-step word problems using the four operations. Represent these problems using equations with a letter standing for the unknown quantity. Assess the reasonableness of answers using mental computation and estimation strategies including rounding.

Key Concepts

In this lesson, students solve this problem:

Jasper wants to buy a microscope. The microscope costs $60. Jasper has saved $24. Then his aunt gives him some money for his birthday. Now Jasper still needs $6 to buy the microscope. How much money does Jasper’s aunt give him?

There are many ways to think about the equation for this problem. One possible word equation is:

total cost of microscope = amount Jasper has now + amount he still needs

If n is the amount Jasper’s aunt gave him, then the amount Jasper has now is 24 + n. So, the equation is:

60 = (24 + n) + 6 or 60 = 24 + n + 6

Another possible word equation is:

total cost of microscope – amount Jasper has saved – amount his aunt gives him = amount he still needs

This leads to the equation:

60 – 24 – n = 6

A third possible word equation is:

total cost of microscope – amount Jasper has so far = amount he still needs

In this case, the “amount Jasper has so far” is the sum 24 + n, so the equation is:

60 − (24 + n) = 6

The first two equations can be solved by doing all the possible calculations and then using the inverse relationship between addition and subtraction. Note that in the first solution, the commutative property of addition is used to reorder the terms.

  • First solution.

    60 = 24 + n + 6

  • 60 = 24 + 6 + n

  • 60 = 30 + n

  • 60 − 30 = n

  • 30 = n

  • Second solution.

    60 − 24 − n = 6

  • 36 − n = 6

  • 36 − 6 = n

  • 30 = n

The last equation, 60 − (24 + n) = 6, can be solved by using the relationship between inverse operations twice. The first time, students must think of 24 + n as a “chunk” that can be manipulated as if it were a single number.

60 − (24 + n) = 6

60 − 6 = 24 + n

54 = 24 + n

54 − 24 = n

30 = n

Goals and Learning Objectives

  • Write equations to find unknown values in word problems

  • Express two-step problem situations using one equation

  • Write two-step equations that do not require parentheses due to the order of operations

  • Solve a simple equation in which the unknown is not alone on one side

  • Model problem situations

  • Estimate solutions

  • Check answers using the relationship between operations
