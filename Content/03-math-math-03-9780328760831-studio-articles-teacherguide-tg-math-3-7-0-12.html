GALLERY LESSONS 12-14

Gallery Overview

Allow students who have a clear understanding of the content thus far in the unit to work on Gallery problems of their choosing. You can then use this time to provide additional help to students who need review of the unit's concepts or to assist students who may have fallen behind on work.

Wire Shapes

Chan used some wire to make shapes. Students know the length of the wire she used. They are challenged to figure out the length of each side of her shapes.


Sharing or Grouping

Students will sort some math stories into two groups: (1) math stories in which the answer tells how many groups, and (2) math stories in which the answer tells how many are in each group. Then students will solve each math story.


Number Puzzle

Students use the numbers in the puzzle to write six equations. They will use each number only once in each equation.


Two Math Stories

Students write and illustrate math stories. First, they write any division equation. Next, they write a math story in which the divisor of their equation stands for the number of groups. Then they write a story in which the divisor stands for the number in each group. Students solve and illustrate each of the math stories.


Who Gets More?

In this task, students do not do any arithmetic. Instead, they think about two sharing situations, and by reasoning about the size of the divisor and the dividend, students decide which situation gives each person a larger share.


Beaded Hair

Students help Kentasha figure out how many beads she can put on each braid.


Grape Juice

First students make a plan about how to solve a problem. Then they put the plan into action. Students’ job is to figure out how many days some juice boxes will last if Mrs. Williams puts one box of juice into each of her children’s lunches each day.


Zoe’s Apples

Students will solve a math story and show their calculation. Then they will use the same numbers from the calculation to write a division equation. Finally, they will write a math story to go with the division equation they made up.

Who Gets More?

Do not do any actual calculations. Figure out who gets more by thinking about the size of the dividend and the size of the divisor in each situation.

Eight boys have 24 figs. They will share them equally. Six girls have 24 figs. They will share them equally.

  • ✓ Who gets more figs, each girl or each boy? Explain why.

Six girls have 18 cherries. They will share them equally.

Six boys have 24 cherries. They will share them equally.

  • ✓ Who gets more cherries, each girl or each boy? Explain why.

Answers

  1. The boys have 24 figs and so do the girls. But there are fewer girls sharing the same number of figs, so each girl will get more figs than each boy.

  2. The same number of girls as boys share the cherries. But the boys have more cherries to share, so each boy will get more cherries than each girl.
