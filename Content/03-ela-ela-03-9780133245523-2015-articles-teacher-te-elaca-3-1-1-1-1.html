Opening

Task 1: Introduction to Unit 1

    • Distribute devices and explain that students will have time to explore them later. For now, they should locate the Opening page of Lesson 1. Explain that this page will tell them what to do.

    • Ask students to watch the opening video for the unit.

      Ask students to explain the words and terms related to community to you to demonstrate their understanding. Asking students to identify the different communities of which they are members can help make the activity more meaningful to them. In turn, personal connections will help them to remember what they have learned.

      Emerging P1.3.1

      Pause the video frequently to check for understanding and to provide clarification. Activate the closed captions to run along the bottom of the screen as additional support. Allow Emerging ELLs to view the video again later (multiple times as needed) for further support. Ask them to name two groups they saw in the video. Ask yes-no and wh- questions:

      • Did anyone work together?
      • Who worked together?

      Expanding P1.3.1

      Activate closed captions to run at the bottom of the screen for Expanding ELLs to read as they watch. Additionally, allow ELLs to view the video again later if you realize they need another opportunity to understand it fully. Ask them to name three groups from the video and to describe what they were doing together. Provide sentence frames:

      • In the video, I saw the groups _____, _____, and _____.
      • They were doing _____.

      Bridging P1.3.1

      Have Bridging ELLs Turn and Talk after the video has played fully (no pauses). Ask students to describe the neighborhood, the people, and the activities they saw in the video using complete sentences with details. Ask what the groups had in common.

      ELL Task Preparation

      Prepare sentence frames for ELLs to use in Tasks 1 and 3.

    • Define community with students. Steer studentsâ€™ thinking of community from a neighborhood concept to a broader application of the term as a group of people who share similar interests, language, or culture. (Community: A group of people who may live close together, a group that shares common interests, or a group who knows each other through work.)

    • Introduce the first Guiding Question as part of the discussion:

      What are some things a community or group can do together that an individual cannot do alone?

      • Students may answer that a group can help each other in times of need, such as after a fire. They may say that a group can help members become better at what they do by sharing skills and expertise.

    • Give one or two examples of community (e.g., the school community, a church community, a business community, a community of gardeners, writers, mystery readers, or train enthusiasts).

    • Emphasize that this year their third grade class will be a community of readers who will share their tastes in reading with each other.
