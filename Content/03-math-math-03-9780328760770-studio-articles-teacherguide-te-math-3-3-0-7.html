LESSON 7: Threes Multiplication Table

Lesson Overview

Students construct the Threes Multiplication Table by building a group of 3 and replicating iterations of that group of 3. Students represent each of the facts in the Threes Multiplcaiton Table by building a model, writing a multiplication equation, and filling in blanks in a sentence. Students look for patterns in the multiplies of 3.

5. Apply properties of operations as strategies to multiply and divide. Examples: If 6 × 4 = 24 is known, then 4 × 6 = 24 is also known. (Commutative property of multiplication.) 3 × 5 × 2 can be found by 3 × 5 = 15, then 15 × 2 = 30, or by 5 × 2 = 10, then 3 × 10 = 30. (Associative property of multiplication.) Knowing that 8 × 5 = 40 and 8 × 2 = 16, one can find 8 × 7 as 8 × (5 + 2) = (8 × 5) + (8 × 2) = 40 + 16 = 56. (Distributive property.)

Key Concepts

Students should notice that when the number of groups increases by 1, the product increases by the group size, which is 3 in this lesson. They should also notice that when the number of groups decreases by 1, the product decreases by the group size.

Students repeat the two’s chant during the Opening. Be sure to find a few minutes outside of math class to have students recite the twos and fives chants as well.

Key Concepts

A rectangular array is an arrangement of objects in rows and columns. Rows are horizontal arrangements of objects in straight lines. Columns are vertical arrangements of objects in straight lines. In an array, all rows have an equal number of objects in them, and all columns have an equal number of objects in them.

Arrays are introduced in this lesson for two reasons.

  • First, to continue developing students’ understanding that the multiplicand is a unit that can be counted (The rows in an array can be counted; all of the individual objects do not have to be counted.)

  • Second, to pave the way for beginning to investigate the commutative property

(An array with 3 rows and 4 columns, 3×4 , can be rotated 90 degrees so that it becomes an array with 4 rows and 3 columns, 4×3 . The convention is that the first factor counts the number of rows and the second factor counts the number of columns.)
