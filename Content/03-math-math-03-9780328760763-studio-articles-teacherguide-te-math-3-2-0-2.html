LESSON 2: More Patterns

Lesson Overview

Students will use a pattern in the addition table to develop the constant-difference strategy for subtraction. Then, they will use the idea of turn-arounds to fill in empty cells in an addition table.

Content Standards

3.OA.9—Operations and Algebraic Thinking

Solve problems involving the four operations, and identify and explain patterns in arithmetic.

9. Identify arithmetic patterns (including patterns in the addition table or multiplication table), and explain them using properties of operations. For example, observe that 4 times a number is always even, and explain why 4 times a number can be decomposed into two equal addends.

Key Concepts

The constant-difference strategy for subtraction involves adding the same number to or subtracting the same number from both the minuend and subtrahend to get a problem that is easy to solve mentally. Usually this involves changing the subtrahend to a multiple of 10.

For example, consider the problem 43 - 18. We can add 2 to both the minuend and subtrahend to get 45 - 20, which is easy to solve mentally. Because we have added 2 to both numbers, the difference is the same.

“Turn-arounds” are pairs of addition equations with the same addends, but in a different order. Below are some examples:

  3+1=4   and 1+3=4

  7+2=9  and 2+7=9

  8+4=12   and 4+8=12

These pairs of sums are equal because of the Commutative Property of Addition, which states that changing the order of the addends in an addition expression does not change the sum.

In the addition table, the cells for a pair of turn-arounds are mirror images over the diagonal that goes from the top left to the bottom right. In this lesson, students use this idea to fill in empty cells in an extended addition table.

Goals and Learning Objectives

  • Identify patterns in the addition table

  • Understand and apply the constant-difference strategy for subtraction

  • Use turn-arounds to complete the addition table
