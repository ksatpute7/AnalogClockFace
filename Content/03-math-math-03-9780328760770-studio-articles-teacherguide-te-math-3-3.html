Type of Unit: Concept

Length of Unit: 15 days

Prior Knowledge

Students should be able to:

  • Use properties of operations to add and subtract.

  • Use addition to find the total number of objects arranged in rectangular arrays and write equations to express the total as a sum of equal addends.

Lesson Flow

The Exploratory lesson sets the stage for the unit by engaging students in finding various ways to represent the number of fingers in their class. The next lesson focuses on the special role of each factor and on using multiplication to represent situations. Lessons 3 and 4 are devoted to a study of the fives facts. Thereafter, students spend two days on each of these multiplication tables: twos, threes, and fours. The first day of studying a set of facts begins with building that multiplication table. Students look for structure, both in the multiplication table and in a chart by shading multiples. They begin to develop an understanding that as the number of groups increases by one, the product increases by the number in each group. Students begin the second day of each two-day focus by reciting a multiplication facts chant. They also develop understanding of the types of situations in which multiplication is useful. As the lessons progress, students encounter multiplication in the form of equal groups, equal lengths, equal distances, and arrays. On the second day of each two-day focus, students also practice writing and illustrating multiplication stories.

While the focus of the unit is on understanding multiplication conceptually, students are also expected to attain fluency with the multiplication facts. By the end of the unit, students should be fluent with the fives, twos, threes, and fours. Knowledge of these facts and of the properties will be important prerequisites for the Multiplying 6, 7, 8 and 9 and Mastering Multiplication units.

  2. Unit Overview 9

  1. Lesson 1: How Many Fingers? 10

    Students will find different ways to represent the number of fingers in the class. Students will learn that the number of objects in equal-sized groups can be represented with multiplication expressions. Then students will practice writing addition and multiplication expressions for equal-sized groups.

  2. Lesson 2: What Is Multiplication? 20

    Students will learn the meaning of each factor in a multiplication expression and understand when multiplication can and cannot be used.

  3. Lesson 3: Fives Multiplication Table 30

    Students will build models of groups of 5 and connect those models to the fives facts. Students will learn to think of “5 flowers in a vase” as a single group of 5.

  4. Lesson 4: Groups of Five 41

    Students will begin memorizing the fives facts. Students will use models to investigate lengths of equal size, and students will look at how the product changes when the number of groups goes up by 1.

  5. Lesson 5: Twos Multiplication Table 51

    Students will build models of groups of 2 and connect those models to the twos facts. Students will learn to think of “2 people in a car” as a single group of 2.

  6. Lesson 6: Groups of Two 63

    Students will begin memorizing the twos facts. Students will consider a special case of the distributive property: when the number of groups goes up by 1, the total goes up by the size of the group.

  7. Lesson 7: Threes Multiplication Table 73

    Students will build models of groups of 3 and connect those models to the threes facts. Students will learn to think of “3 tennis balls” as a single group of 3.

  8. Lesson 8: Groups of Three 85

    Students will begin memorizing the threes facts. Students will look at what happens to the total number of objects in an array when rows of 3 are added or removed.

  9. Lesson 9: Fours Multiplication Table 96

    Students will build models of groups of 4 and connect those models to the fours facts. Students will learn to think of “4 apples in each box” as a single group of 4. Students will consider a special case of the distributive property: how does the product change when there is 1 more box of apples?

  10. Lesson 10: Groups of Four 106

    Students will begin memorizing the fours facts. Students will begin to think about the commutative property by examining two arrays:3 × 4 and 4 × 3.

  11. Lesson 11: Putting It Together 116

    Students will work with a partner to complete a card sort. As students work, they will make connections between multiplication and repeated addition. Students will connect arrays and pictures of equal groups to addition and multiplication expressions.

  12. Lessons 12–14: Gallery 123

    Students who have a clear understanding of the content thus far in the unit will work on Gallery problems of their choosing.

  13. Lesson 15: Unit Assessment 127

    Students will find out what they have learned about multiplication.

Common Core State Standards for Mathematics

This unit focuses on the following Common Core State Standards for Mathematics:

3.OA—Operations and Algebraic Thinking

Represent and solve problems involving multiplication and division.

  • 1. Interpret products of whole numbers, e.g., interpret 5 × 7 as the total number of objects in 5 groups of 7 objects each. For example, describe a context in which a total number of objects can be expressed as 5 × 7.

  • 3. Use multiplication and division within 100 to solve word problems in situations involving equal groups, arrays, and measurement quantities, e.g., by using drawings and equations with a symbol for the unknown number to represent the problem.1

Understand properties of multiplication and the relationship between multiplication and division.

  • 5. Apply properties of operations as strategies to multiply and divide.1 Examples: If 6 × 4 = 24 is known, then 4 × 6 = 24 is also known. (Commutative property of multiplication.) 3 × 5 × 2 can be found by 3 × 5 = 15, then 15 × 2 = 30, or by 5 × 2 = 10, then 3 × 10 = 30. (Associative property of multiplication.) Knowing that 8 × 5 = 40 and 8 × 2 = 16, one can find 8 × 7 as 8 × (5 + 2) = (8 × 5) + (8 × 2) = 40 + 16 = 56. (Distributive property.)

Solve problems involving the four operations, and identify and explain patterns in arithmetic.

  • 9. Identify arithmetic patterns (including patterns in the addition table or multiplication table), and explain them using properties of operations. For example, observe that 4 times a number is always even, and explain why 4 times a number can be decomposed into two equal addends.

  4. Model with mathematics.
