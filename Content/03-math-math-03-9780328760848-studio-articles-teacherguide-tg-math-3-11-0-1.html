LESSON 1: Exploring Volume

Lesson Overview

Students are introduced to measuring and comparing liquid volumes using nonstandard units. They measure the volume of two containers using two types of measuring cups, and then measure and compare the volume of three containers.

Content Standards

3.MD—Measurement and Data

Solve problems involving measurement and estimation of intervals of time, liquid volumes, and masses of objects.

  • 2. Measure and estimate liquid volumes and masses of objects using standard units of grams (g), kilograms (kg), and liters (l). Add, subtract, multiply, or divide to solve one-step word problems involving masses or volumes that are given in the same units, e.g., by using drawings (such as a beaker with a measurement scale) to represent the problem.

Key Concepts

Measuring the volume of a container involves choosing a unit of measure, and determining how many “copies” of that unit are needed to fill the container. When comparing the volume of two containers, the measuring unit must be the same in order for the comparison to make sense.

Goals and Learning Objectives

  • Develop an understanding that to meaningfully compare volumes of two different containers, you must use the same unit of measure.

Materials

For Task 3:

  • Two containers of different sizes (one set for each group of four students; see Preparation)

  • Two measuring cups of different sizes (one set for each group of four students; see Preparation)

  • Water to fill containers—alternatively, you could use dry materials such as rice or lentils (enough so that each group of four can fill both of the two containers)

For Task 5:

  • One measuring cup and three containers in assorted sizes—such as yogurt containers, oatmeal cartons, cookie tins (one for each group of four students)

  • Water to fill containers—alternatively, you could use dry materials such as rice or lentils (enough so that each group of four can fill all three containers)

Preparation

For Task 3:

  • When choosing containers and measuring cups, make sure that each container can be filled by an even number of either of the measuring cups. In addition, choose containers and measuring cups such that when you fill the larger container with the smaller measuring cup, you need to use at least two or three more cups than when you fill the smaller container with the larger measuring cup. Possible sizes: container 1 - 12 oz; container 2 - 16 oz; measuring cup A - 1/2 cup; measuring cup B - 1/4 cup.

  • Label the smaller containers “1” and the larger containers “2.”

  • Label the larger measuring cups “A” and the smaller measuring cups “B.”

  • Complete Task 3 yourself so you know the correct answers.

For Task 5:

  • When choosing containers, try to find containers for which a taller container has less volume than a shorter container. Once again, choose containers that students can fill using an even number of measuring cups.

  • It is not necessary that all groups get identical containers.
