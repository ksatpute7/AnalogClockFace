Type of Unit: Concept

Length of Unit: 15 days

Prior Knowledge

  • Know from memory the twos, threes, fours, and fives

  • Know the meaning of factors (first factor counts number of groups and second factor counts the number of objects in each group)

  • Recognize equal groups situations as multiplication situations

  • Know that when the number of groups increases by 1 the product increases by the number in each group

Lesson Flow

The Exploratory lesson sets the stage for the unit by engaging students in finding patterns in a multiplication table. The lesson helps students notice patterns that are based on the distributive and commutative properties. Students use these patterns throughout the unit as they build the sixes, sevens, eights, and nines.

The unit is structured much like Unit 3.3, with two days devoted to studying each multiplicand. The first day of studying begins with building that multiplication table. Students use patterns to build up each set of facts and they describe the patterns that they used. The second day spent on each set of facts deepens students understanding of multiplication by involving them in investigating the properties of operations. On some days the focus is on using the distributive property and on other days students work with the commutative property.

While the focus of the unit is on understanding multiplication conceptually, students are also expected to know from memory the products of all single digit numbers. Each day begins with students chanting one of the multiplication chants. Some students may need more repetition than can be accommodated in math class. Chanting on the way to recess or lunch or asking students to teach the chants to a family member can provide that additional practice. By the end of the unit, students should be fluent with all facts from 1 x 2 to 9 x 9. Knowledge of these facts and of the properties will be important prerequisites for Unit 3.6.

Contents

  1. Lesson 1: Patterns in Multiplication 8

    Students review the products learned in Unit 3.3 by repeating a multiplication chant. They examine a multiplication table showing only the products studied in Unit 3.3. They look for patterns in these products and use the patterns to find products they have not yet learned.

  2. Lesson 2: The Sixes 16

    Students review the products learned in Unit 3.3 by repeating a multiplication chant. Students use the patterns they noticed in Lesson 1 to build the sixes multiplication table. They use properties to solve problems in two different ways.

  3. Lesson 3: Groups of 6 and 6 Groups 28

    Students begin memorizing the sixes by repeating a multiplication chant. They work with arrays to understand the commutative property.

  4. Lesson 4: The Sevens 38

    Students continue memorizing the sixes by repeating a multiplication chant. Students use the patterns they noticed in Lesson 1 to build the sevens multiplication table. They use properties to solve a problem in two different ways.

  5. Lesson 5: Groups of 7 and 7 Groups 51

    Students begin memorizing the sevens by repeating a multiplication chant. Students investigate what happens to the product when the multiplicand goes up by 1 (i.e., when the group size increases by 1).

  6. Lesson 6: The Eights 61

    Students continue memorizing the sevens by repeating a multiplication chant. Students use the patterns they noticed in Lesson 1 to build the eights multiplication table. Students continue to investigate what happens to the product when the multiplicand is increased by 1.

  7. Lesson 7: Groups of 8 and 8 Groups 73

    Students begin memorizing the eights by repeating a multiplication chant. Students use properties to solve a problem in two different ways.

  8. Lesson 8: The Nines 82

    Students continue memorizing the eights by repeating a multiplication chant. Students use the patterns they noticed in Lesson 1 to build the nines multiplication table. They use properties to solve problems in different ways.

  9. Lesson 9: Groups of 9 and 9 Groups 92

    Students begin memorizing the nines by repeating a multiplication chant. They use the associative and commutative properties to solve problems.

  10. Lesson 10: Multiplying Three Factors 101

    Students continue memorizing the nines by repeating a multiplication chant. Students use the associative and commutative properties to solve problems involving three factors. Students also complete a pre-assessment (Self Check problems).

  11. Lesson 11: Putting It Together 112

    Students review the Self Check problem. They solve word problems in multiple ways, using the properties of multiplication.

  12. Lessons 12–14: Gallery 123

    Students who have a clear understanding of the content thus far in the unit will work on Gallery problems of their choosing.

  13. Lesson 15: Unit Assessment 128

    The final assessment is computer scored items and performance tasks.

Common Core State Standards for Mathematics

This unit focuses on the following Common Core State Standards for Mathematics:

3.OA—Operations and Algebraic Thinking

Represent and solve problems involving multiplication and division.

1. Interpret products of whole numbers, e.g., interpret 5 x 7 as the total number of objects in 5 groups of 7 objects each. For example, describe a context in which a total number of objects can be expressed as 5 x 7.

3. Use multiplication and division within 100 to solve word problems in situations involving equal groups, arrays, and measurement quantities, e.g., by using drawings and equations with a symbol for the unknown number to represent the problem.

Understand properties of multiplication and the relationship between multiplication and division.

5. Apply properties of operations as strategies to multiply and divide. Examples: If 6 x 4 = 24 is known, then 4 x 6 = 24 is also known. (Commutative property of multiplication.) 3 x 5 x 2 can be found by 3 x 5 = 15, then 15 x 2 = 30, or by 5 x 2 = 10, then 3 x 10 = 30. (Associative property of multiplication.) Knowing that 8 x 5 = 40 and 8 x 2 = 16, one can find 8 x 7 as 8 x (5 + 2) = (8 x 5) + (8 x 2) = 40 + 16 = 56. (Distributive property.)

Solve problems involving the four operations, and identify and explain patterns in arithmetic.

9. Identify arithmetic patterns (including patterns in the addition table or multiplication table), and explain them using properties of operations. For example, observe that 4 times a number is always even, and explain why 4 times a number can be decomposed into two equal addends.

Mathematical Practices

  1. Make sense of problems and persevere in solving them.

  2. Reason abstractly and quantitatively.

  3. Construct viable arguments and critique the reasoning of others.

  4. Model with mathematics.

  5. Use appropriate tools strategically.

  6. Attend to precision.

  7. Look for and make use of structure.

  8. Look for and express regularity in repeated reasoning.
