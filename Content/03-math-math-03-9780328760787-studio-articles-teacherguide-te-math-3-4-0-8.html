LESSONS 8-9: Gallery

Gallery Overview

Allow students who have a clear understanding of the content thus far in the unit to work on Gallery problems of their choosing. You can then use this time to provide additional help to students who need review of the unit's concepts or to assist students who may have fallen behind on work.

Gallery Descriptions

Points of a Square

Students are given two grids, each with two marked points that represent the vertices (corners) of a square. Students mark two more points on each grid that could be the two vertices of the square.

Points of a Rectangle

Students are given two grids, each with two marked points that represent the vertices (corners) of a rectangle. Students mark two points on each grid that could be the other two vertices of the rectangle.

Perimeter of a Rectangle

A square has a perimeter of 12 units. A rectangle is as wide as the square, but it is 4 times as long. Students find the perimeter of the rectangle.

Quadrilaterals and Triangles

If a quadrilateral is divided into two triangles, are they always the same? When are they different? Students explore a sketch and find out.

Kites

What kind of quadrilateral has two pairs of sides that are the same length but is not a rectangle? This type of quadrilateral is called a kite. Students explore a sketch of a kite to learn more about them.

What's My Perimeter?

Students can find the perimeter of any closed shape by adding the side lengths. Students investigate other shapes to find their perimeters.

Quadrilateral Sorting

Are squares rectangles? Students sort different quadrilaterals using a Venn diagram to show which shapes have certain attributes.

Is It a Square?

Students are given six different shapes and have to decide which ones are squares and explain why.

Find the Squares

On a square grid, students connect points to form squares.

Points of a Square

Possible Answers

1.

Treating the given points as opposite vertices

The first grid presents an interesting challenge because the given points do not fall on intersections of grid lines, and students may be used to seeing points only at intersections. One way to locate two other vertices is to count the number of units between the given points. Because there are six grid squares between given points and all four line segments of a square are congruent, the other two vertices must be six units from the given vertices. To correctly place the vertices, students must understand that the distance from one intersection to the adjacent intersection is the same as the distance from one midpoint to the adjacent midpoint.

On the second grid, the line connecting the given points is a diagonal line. To complete a square in which the given points are adjacent vertices, students must draw diagonal lines that are the same length as the diagonal line that connects the given points.

Treating the given points as adjacent vertices

On the first grid, to make a square in which the given points form opposite vertices, extend diagonal lines from each given vertex until they meet. (In this solution, the left vertex is slightly off the grid.)

On the second grid, to make a square in which the given points form opposite vertices, first draw a vertical line up from the lower dot and a horizontal line left from the upper dot. Then draw a vertical line down from the upper dot and a horizontal line right from the lower dot. The missing vertices are located where the lines intersect.

Points of a Rectangle

Possible Answers

In each grid, the given points are arranged so that they can be seen as adjacent vertices—connected to each other by a line segment—or as opposite vertices.

1.

Placing the other two vertices is much easier if the given points are seen as opposite vertices. The other two vertices can be found by drawing a vertical line up from the lower dot and a horizontal line right from the upper dot, and then drawing a vertical line down from the upper dot and a horizontal line left from the lower dot. The missing vertices are located where the lines intersect.

Viewing the given points as adjacent vertices allows for more than one possible rectangle, but correct placement of the vertices is more difficult. Start by connecting the given dots in each grid with a line segment. Then draw lines that intersect the connecting line segment at 90° angles to each of the given vertices. Now any line that runs parallel to the original line and intersects the lines that run at 90° angles to the original line will finish a rectangle. The points where the lines meet are the rectangle’s missing vertices. The answer here shows one possible placement of the vertices for each grid using this view of the given points.

Perimeter of a Rectangle

Answer

  1. The rectangle has a perimeter of 30 units.

  2. Because the perimeter of the square is 12 units and 4 x 3 = 12, one side must be 3 units long.

    By measuring the one side of the square and the length and width of the rectangle, you can find that the rectangle is the same width as the square and 4 times as long.

    12 + 12 + 3 + 3 = 30

    The perimeter of the rectangle is 30 units long.

Quadrilaterals and Triangles

Possible Answers

Students will become familiar with the sketch.

  1. The two triangles always have different sizes or shapes. The directions also include the restriction that the sides next to each other are not the same length because if they were, the student could draw a kite.

  2. The triangles are always the same size and shape.

  3. If a quadrilateral cannot be divided into two triangles that are the same size and shape, then it is not a rectangle, square, or rhombus and is most likely only a quadrilateral. However, the quadrilateral could be a trapezoid or a kite. It is fine if students make either discovery and they can think about how these figures have other characteristics than those of “plain” quadrilaterals.

    If a quadrilateral can be divided into two triangles that are the same size and shape, it is most likely a rectangle, square, or rhombus.

Kites

Answers

  1. As students become familiar with the shapes, they should notice that two pairs of adjacent sides remain the same length.

  2. Yes, a rhombus can be made if both pairs of adjacent sides are the same length.

  3. A kite is a quadrilateral with two pairs of sides next to each other that are the same length.

    Some texts define a kite as a quadrilateral with two pairs of adjacent sides of different lengths. This definition avoids the categorization of rhombuses as kites.

  4. Depending on how the student defines kite, a rhombus could be a kite in the same way that a square is a rectangle.
