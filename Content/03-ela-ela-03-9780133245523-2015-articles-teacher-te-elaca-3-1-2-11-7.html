Task 7: Grammar and Language: Concrete vs. Abstract Nouns

    • Discuss the difference between concrete and abstract nouns. Explain that some nouns are easy to identify, such as rocks, paper, and scissors, because you can see and touch these things. These nouns are called concrete. Explain that some things you can’t see and touch, like happiness or leadership, are also nouns, and that these nouns are abstract.

    • Say to students:

      • You just did some thinking, had a discussion, then wrote in your Reading Log. Is discussion concrete or abstract? What about thinking? What about the Reading Log? Only the Reading Log is concrete, because it is an object. (However, if students’ Reading Logs are on a tablet or other device, change the setup to “then you used your tablet [or name of device] to write something” and make the device name the third noun.)

    • If time allows or more exposure is necessary to help students differentiate these types of nouns, play the Thumbs-Up, Thumbs-Down game, using words such as banana, toy, hope, lightbulb, truth, chalk, toaster, crime.

    • Say to students:

      • Let’s look at nouns that name some of the things we do and use each day.

    • Ask the class:

      • Is a feeling, like joy or loneliness, concrete or abstract? (abstract)
      • We do a lot of thinking in our class. Are thoughts concrete or abstract? (abstract)
      • Is your Reading Log concrete or abstract? (concrete)
