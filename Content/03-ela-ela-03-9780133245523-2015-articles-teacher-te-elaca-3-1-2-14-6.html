Work Time

Task 6: Interview Report

    • While students are waiting for a reply, project the Grade 3 Informational Writing Rubric.

    • Explain that reviewing the rubric before writing helps them plan what elements to include in their work. Review each element of the rubric and clarify terms.

      Emerging P1.3.6

      Conduct a close reading of the rubric. Create a symbol for each rubric category, and draw it on the rubric. Pose yes-no and wh- questions to ensure Emerging ELLs understand the elements expected of them.

      Expanding P1.3.6

      Conduct a close reading of the rubric. Create a symbol for each rubric category, and draw it on the rubric. The symbols for each rubric category will help Expanding ELLs make connections between the expectations and their own writing. Ask students questions about the rubric to ensure an understanding of all the components.

      Bridging P1.3.6

      Conduct a close reading of the rubric. Have Bridging ELLs draw a symbol for each category on their rubric. Have students paraphrase the components of the rubric in their own words.

    • Remind students that the choice of an organizing structure or structures is a key decision in report writing. The organizing structure for this report will be a paragraph structure. They will write two paragraphs: one that will give a description of the community member’s work and one that will tell about how that job helps the school to run smoothly.

    • Explain that the rubric can also be used as a tool for evaluating their work and that they will use the rubric after their piece is drafted.

    • Display a model email response from a community member. Model how to use a response from the interview email to inform the writing. The answer to each question should be incorporated into the report. (For example, if the question is “What is your name?” and the answer is “My name is Mr. Jones,” you could write “The person I interviewed is named Mr. Jones.”)

    • After modeling one or two questions, ask students to read another of your sample questions and answers and turn it into a statement for the report. Check that they understand the process.

    • Have students practice changing a response to a question into a statement for their own report with any information that they have already compiled, such as the person’s name and job title.

      Make sure that students have clear and specific guidelines for the report. Some students with disabilities may not as easily know what they need to complete to meet this assignment’s expectations. Provide a checklist to keep them on the right track.
