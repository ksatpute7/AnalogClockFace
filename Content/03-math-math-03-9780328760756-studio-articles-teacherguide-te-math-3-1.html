UNIT 1

Tell the Exact Time

Type of Unit: Concept

Length of Unit: 8 days

Prior Knowledge

Students should be able to:

  • Read number words for numbers to 100

  • Tell and write time from analog and digital clocks to the nearest 5 minutes, using a.m. and p.m.

  • Partition circles into two and four equal shares, describe the shares using the words halves and quarters

  • Use the expressions for time, such as “o’clock” and “half past,” when telling time

  • Know how many hours are in a day, in the a.m. hours, and in the p.m. hours

  • Use addition and subtraction within 100 to solve one- and two-step word problems

Lesson Flow

In contexts related to everyday life, this unit extends students’ skills in telling and writing time using analog and digital clocks and develops students’ understanding of elapsed time as a quantity between one time and another. In the Exploratory lesson, students use what they know about telling time to the nearest 5 minutes to read an analog clock for an exact time. Students then investigate what all the tick marks on an analog clock mean and learn to tell and write time in hours and minutes.

The remainder of the unit develops students’ ability to solve problems involving elapsed time. In Lesson 2, students are introduced to a timeline and learn to find time intervals less than or equal to an hour when given a start time and an end time. In Lesson 3, students use the timeline to find time intervals less than and greater than an hour when given a start time and an end time. In Lesson 4, students use the timeline to find either the start time or the end time when given a time and the elapsed time. In Lesson 5, students add and subtract time intervals to solve problems. Lesson 6 is the Putting It Together lesson: Students perform a card sort in which they match cards about a math story involving elapsed time. Lesson 7 is the Gallery, in which a range of tasks like those in the unit are provided. The unit ends with a Unit Assessment.

Contents

  • Lesson 1: What Time Is It? 7

    What do the big and small tick marks on an analog clock mean? Solve problems by reading clocks. Show the time on an analog clock. Tell time to the hour and minute.

  • Lesson 2: How Many Minutes? 12

    Watch a video about a class field trip. How long did students spend at the penguins? Use a number line for time, called a “timeline,” to find how many minutes have gone by

  • Lesson 3: How Many Minutes Elapsed? 19

    Solve more problems about elapsed time. Learn different ways to find out how many minutes have gone by.

  • Lesson 4: What Time Will It Be? 27

    Find the start time or the end time when given a time and the number of minutes that have gone by.

  • Lesson 5: Time Intervals 36

    Solve math stories about time. Complete a Self Check task.

  • Lesson 6: Putting It Together
    36

    Sort the cards to show an elapsed time math story. Revise your work on the Self Check task.

  • Lesson 7: Gallery 53

    • Students who have a clear understanding of the content thus far in the unit will work on Gallery problems of their choosing.

  • Lesson 8: Unit Assessment 53

    Take an assessment to find out what you have learned about time.

Common Core State Standards for Mathematics

This unit focuses on the following Common Core State Standards for Mathematics:

  • 3.MD—Measurement and Data

  • Solve problems involving measurement and estimation of intervals of time, liquid volumes, and masses of objects.

    • 1. Tell and write time to the nearest minute and measure time intervals in minutes. Solve word problems involving addition and subtraction of time intervals in minutes, e.g., by representing the problem on a time line.

Mathematical Practices

  1. Make sense of problems and persevere in solving them.

  2. Reason abstractly and quantitatively.

  3. Construct viable arguments and critique the reasoning of others.

  4. Model with mathematics.

  5. Use appropriate tools strategically.

  6. Attend to precision.

  7. Look for and make use of structure.

  8. Look for and express regularity in repeated reasoning.
