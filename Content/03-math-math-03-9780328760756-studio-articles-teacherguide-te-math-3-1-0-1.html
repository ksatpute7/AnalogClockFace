LESSON 1: What Time Is It?

Lesson Overview

What do the big and small tick marks on an analog clock mean? Solve problems by reading clocks. Show the time on an analog clock. Tell time to the hour and minute.

Content Standards

3. MD—Measurement and Data

Solve problems involving measurement and estimation of intervals of time, liquid volumes, and masses of objects.

1. Tell and write time to the nearest minute and measure time intervals in minutes. Solve word problems involving addition and subtraction of time intervals in minutes, e.g., by representing the problem on a time line.

Key Concepts

In earlier grades, students learned to tell time, first to the hour and half hour and then to the nearest 5 minutes. In Grade 3, students tell time to the minute. Learning to read 5-minute times facilitates learning to read 1-minute times: Students count up or down from a big tick mark (a 5-minute mark) rather than count by Is from the top of the clock.

To read the time on an analog clock, a person first reads the hour and then reads the minutes. Reading the hour requires interpreting the hour hand when it is pointing to a location between two hour numbers. When students are learning to tell time, they often incorrectly follow the point of the hour hand and name the nearest hour number.

To read the hour correctly, students need to think of the space between adjacent hour numbers as a region, or portion, of the clock. All the positions within the region correspond to the current hour, which is given by the number preceding the hour hand: If the hour hand is between 4 and 5, the hour is 4.

Reading the minutes involves following the point of the hand. The reader then identifies the big or small tick mark the minute hand is pointing to. The challenge is that these tick marks are usually unlabeled. Students in Grade 3, having learned to read the unlabeled big tick marks as 5-minute times, now must learn to read the unlabeled small tick marks in order to read 1-minute times. Students use two approaches to read 1-minute times:

  • Recalling the number label from memory

  • Using a counting strategy

There are two effective counting strategies for reading 1-minute times:

  • If the number of minutes at the 5-minute mark is recognized (remembered), students count by 1s up or down from a 5-minute mark.

  • Starting at the top of the clock, students count by 5s to the nearest 5-minute mark and then count up or down by 1s.

Three errors are common when students are learning to read 1-minute times:

  • [common error] The student starts the count in the wrong place. When starting the count at the top of the clock, the student says “5” at the top of the clock instead of saying “5” at the first 5-minute mark (1 on the clock), resulting in a time reading that is off by 5 minutes.

  • [common error] When starting with the time at the nearest 5-minute mark and then counting by 1s, the student incorrectly recalls the time for the 5-minute mark.

  • [common error] When the time is within 1 minute of a 5-minute mark, the student rounds to the 5-minute mark.

Students tend to count by 5s from the hour (the top of the clock) more often for times that are farther away from a 5-minute mark and try to use recall of a 5-minute time for times that are only a minute from a 5-minute mark. As a result, recall errors occur more often for times that are 1 minute from a 5-minute mark.

The digital clock shows the current time written in numeric form. Reading the time from a digital clock is quick; however, the digital clock is not as helpful for learning about time relationships.

Once students learn to tell time to the hour and minute, they should have frequent opportunities to practice reading analog clocks in order to develop the ability to tell time easily and accurately. Having students note the time on the clock during school days provides quick practice and also opportunities to relate time to daily events.

Goals and Learning Objectives

  • Read time to the hour and minute from analog and digital clocks

  • Write time to the minute in standard numeric written format (e.g., 6:48)

Materials

  • Classroom analog clock, optional
