A Closure Exemplar

Listen and follow along as your teacher reads the closure of Gram’s Angel Food Cake.

Turn and Talk with your partner. How did you know it was the end?

The author uses specific phrases to show readers that she is looking back at this time from the future. This is called a reflective ending, and it is something you could try in your narrative.

Turn and Talk with your partner about how you might use a reflective ending strategy to end your own narrative.

Now, imagine that Abi Gotthelf did not write the last page to this narrative. Imagine it ended on the second-to-last page with: “Everyone loved the cake, and no one suspected a thing. Abi got the piece with the hole in it, but she didn’t care. “Look, Gram,” she joked, “I ate the ‘hole’ piece!”

In this ending, the story is over when Gram and Abi deal with the hole in the cake.

This is another strong way to end a narrative: a solution to the narrative’s main problem.

Turn and Talk with your partner about how you might use this strategy to end your narrative.

  • Now, make a decision about which strategy you want to try writing: a reflective ending that looks back from a later time or an ending where the story’s main problem is solved. If you have time, you can experiment with both.
