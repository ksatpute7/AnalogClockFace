Prior Knowledge

Students should be able to:

  • Add and subtract fluently within 1,000.

  • Multiply and divide fluently within 100.

  • Multiply by 10s, 100s, and 1,000s.

Lesson Flow

In Lesson 1 students are introduced to measuring and comparing liquid volumes using nonstandard units. The lesson establishes the need for using standard units in order to make volume comparisons meaningful. Lesson 2 provides students with experience measuring volume in milliliters and liters using a liter cube. In Lesson 3 students use addition and subtraction to solve word problems involving measuring volume.

The next four lessons focus on weight measurement. In Lesson 4 students investigate and compare the weights of common objects. In this lesson they first compare weights by “feel.” Then they use a homemade balance to compare the weights of objects. Students measure using nonstandard units (i.e., dimes and nickels) in Lesson 5 and learn the importance of using standardized measurement units. They then weigh objects in grams and kilograms using a pan balance. In Lessons 6 and 7 students use addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division to solve word problems involving measuring weight in grams and kilograms. In Lesson 7 they also complete two Self Check tasks involving volume and weight.

Lesson 8 is a Putting It Together lesson. Students consolidate what they have learned as they measure both weight and volume, and solve problems based on their measurements. The unit ends with a Gallery lesson in which students choose a task that reviews and/or extends the skills they learned in the unit.

Contents

  1. Lesson 1: Exploring Volume 8

    Students will measure the volume of two containers using two types of measuring cups and then will measure and compare the volume of three containers.

  2. Lesson 2: How Much Does It Hold? 14

    Students will measure the volume of containers using a liter cube.

  3. Lesson 3: Solving Volume Problems 21

    Students will use addition and subtraction to solve word problems involving volume.

  4. Lesson 4: So Heavy! 29

    Students will compare the weights of common objects. They will first compare the weight of objects by feel alone, and then build and use a homemade balance to compare weights.

  5. Lesson 5: Measuring Weight 36

    Students will watch a video in which nonstandard units (i.e., nickels and dimes) are used to weigh an object; they learn the importance of using standardized measurement units. Students will estimate and then measure the weight of various objects in grams and kilograms using a pan balance.

  6. Lesson 6: Solving Weight Problems 43

    Students will use addition and subtraction to solve word problems involving measuring weight in grams and kilograms.

  7. Lesson 7: Weighty Problems

    Students will use addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division to solve word problems involving measuring weight in grams and kilograms. They will complete two Self Check tasks involving volume and weight.

  8. Lesson 8: Putting It Together

    Students will measure the weights and volumes of teacups and coffee mugs and solve problems based on their measurements. Students will also review and revise their work on the Self Check tasks.

  9. Lesson 9: Gallery 66

    Students who have a clear understanding of the content thus far in the unit will will choose one or more Gallery problems to complete about weight and volume.

Common Core State Standards for Mathematics

This unit focuses on the following Common Core State Standards for Mathematics:

3.MD—Measurement and Data

Solve problems involving measurement and estimation of intervals of time, liquid volumes, and masses of objects.

2. Measure and estimate liquid volumes and masses of objects using standard units of grams (g), kilograms (kg), and liters (l). Add, subtract, multiply, or divide to solve one-step word problems involving masses or volumes that are given in the same units, e.g., by using drawings (such as a beaker with a measurement scale) to represent the problem. 

Mathematical Practices

  1. Make sense of problems and persevere in solving them.

  2. Reason abstractly and quantitatively.

  3. Construct viable arguments and critique the reasoning of others.

  4. Model with mathematics.

  5. Use appropriate tools strategically.

  6. Attend to precision.

  7. Look for and make use of structure.

  8. Look for and express regularity in repeated reasoning.
